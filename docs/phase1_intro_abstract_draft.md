# Phase 1 Intro/Abstract Draft (1-Page Equivalent)

## Draft Abstract
Complex systems in neuroscience, epidemiology, social dynamics, and markets often exhibit dependencies that are not well represented by pairwise links alone. In many settings, interactions among triples or larger groups drive critical transitions, motivating higher-order network representations via hypergraphs. When these structures evolve over time under unobserved regime changes, inference becomes a coupled latent-state and sparse-structure problem with substantial identifiability and sample-complexity challenges.

This work studies sparse higher-order dynamical hypergraph recovery under latent Markov regime switching. We consider observations generated from regime-specific nonlinear/high-order operators with sparse hyperedge structure and additive noise, where regime assignments are unobserved and transitions follow a finite-state Markov chain. We formulate inference as variational optimization with structured sparsity and temporal regularization, combining an amortized posterior over regimes with proximal updates for hyperedge parameters.

Our primary contribution is a theorem-oriented framework: (i) identifiability results up to standard symmetry classes (e.g., label permutations and representation-equivalent transforms) under explicit separation, sparsity, incoherence, and mixing assumptions; (ii) finite-sample recovery rates for support and transition parameters that scale polynomially in model dimensions and logarithmically in confidence terms; and (iii) lower-bound constructions indicating near-optimal dependence under the same problem geometry. We additionally provide algorithmic convergence characterizations for the variational-proximal optimization routine under standard smoothness/regularity conditions.

Empirically, we implement an auditable synthetic pipeline with deterministic artifacts, adversarial controls (permutation, null-structure, random-label baselines), and schema-level logic audits to prevent overclaiming. These checks reveal both strengths and failure regimes: pairwise and switching baselines recover planted structure at moderate noise, while nonlinear observability bottlenecks can invalidate naive structural claims in biologically motivated settings, reinforcing the need for principled higher-order latent inference.

Overall, the project establishes a rigorous bridge between sparse inverse problems, latent regime dynamical modeling, and higher-order network learning, with a clear path toward theoretically justified and practically reliable recovery in evolving complex systems.

## Draft Intro Core Paragraph
Higher-order interactions and latent regime shifts are both empirically common and theoretically difficult. Existing work largely addresses them in isolation: sparse graphical models provide static pairwise recovery guarantees; latent-variable decompositions clarify identifiability under confounding; hypergraph models capture high-order geometry but are often static; switching dynamical models characterize latent temporal segmentation but usually remain pairwise. The open gap is their intersection: sparse high-order operators evolving under latent regimes with end-to-end recovery guarantees. Our project targets precisely this intersection with conservative assumptions, explicit failure diagnostics, and theorem-to-experiment alignment.
